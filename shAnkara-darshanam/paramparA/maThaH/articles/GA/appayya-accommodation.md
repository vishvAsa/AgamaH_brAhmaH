Will only mention a few points:

## Śaiva term
When Śaiva is used distinctly but together with pāśupata, Kāpālika, kālāmukha, kāruka, etc, it refers to the Siddhānta. This is a conventional usage. 

## Vaidika-Pāśupatam
Appayya expressly refers—in his Śivatattvaviveka—to Vaidika-Pāśupatam as the acceptable path since it (allegedly) upholds that Śiva is both the material and instrumental cause of the jagat. This would exclude the Saiddhāntika canon. 

## Aśrauta
The reference to an “Aśrauta” Śaiva canon arises in the Vāyavīya (final book of the Śivapurāṇa) but the reference is not at all negative. It is called Aśrauta because it is not tethered to the Veda and has its own independent divine status. This Aśrauta canon is defined in that chapter of the Vāyavīya as Kāmika, etc—again, the Saiddhāntika canon. 

Appayya—again in the Śivatattvaviveka—misinterprets the Aśrauta reference as a negative reference intended to critique the Aśrauta Śaiva canon, that is, the Siddhāntāgama canon. 


## Mangled Vāyavīya
Appayya again misinterprets a śloka of the Vāyavīya as supporting upādānakāraṇatva of Śiva, relying on a chapter that is particularly confusing (likely due to poor quality of scribal copying) when many other chapters in the Vāyavīya make it clear that the Vāyavīya is particularly concordant with the Siddhānta, upholds the rigorous distinction between the sentient and insentient, the idea that Śiva and Śakti are different from the material causes of Māyā and Mahāmāyā (Bindu). 

## Force-fit attempt
With due respect to his great contributions, he was very attached to the abhinna-nimittopādāna-kāraṇa-vāda and sought to force fit all of Śaivam into it, including Saiddhāntika portions of the Vāyavīya, which is an untenable exercise.

## Śivādvaita
While Appayya sought to revive an all-but-dead “Vaidika Pāśupatam” (which took on the form of Śivādvaita after his time and reached some heights) and ignored the Siddhāntāgama, his grand-nephew (Nīlakaṇṭha Dīkṣita) was more fortunate enough to recognize the divine value of the Siddhāntāgama canon and called the Kāmikādi canon “divya”.
